# Voices — six thinkers, read from the inside

This file is a tool. Six thinkers whose work forms research ground for how InsideOut can write about interior work and its relationship to what happens outside a person: Carl Gustav Jung, Gabor Maté, Peter Levine, Debbie Ford, Eckhart Tolle, and Joe Dispenza.

It is research — each voice heard once, carefully, so the seeing they arrived at is available when it is needed.

## The frame

Each of the six has built a vocabulary and a method that, on the surface, differs from the others. Jung speaks in archetypes and projection; Maté in attachment, authenticity, and cultural harm; Levine in autonomic discharge and titration; Ford in shadow and reclamation; Tolle in presence and the pain-body; Dispenza in habituated identity and rehearsed future. Read from the outside, they look like separate disciplines.

Read from the inside — from what each of them has actually seen about how a person is held together, what is underneath a symptom, and how change happens — they touch the same territory more than they diverge from it. The working hypothesis this file was built on is that all six are naming the same thing in different dialects. The research mostly confirms that. A few real navigation points remain, named honestly in the closing section rather than smoothed away, because they are part of the tool.

Each voice is given in the same shape: **seeing** (what the person has seen, in a form available to someone who has to write from it), **quotes** (verified against primary sources where possible), and **edges** (what is robust, what is contested, what to avoid). The closing section holds the synthesis.

## Carl Gustav Jung (1875–1961)

### Seeing

A person is not only what their ego includes. There is a center to a person that is not the ego — Jung called it the Self — and most of a life is organized by material the ego refuses to meet. Whatever is disowned does not stay quiet. It goes out into the world and comes back as circumstance, as other people, as what a person calls their fate. Outer and inner are not two things. The outer is the inner, read from the far side.

The **shadow** is whatever the conscious personality has refused. It contains what the culture told us was unacceptable — which means it contains not only the rage, the envy, the contradictions, but also, as Jung was explicit, "good qualities, normal instincts, appropriate reactions, realistic insights, creative impulses" (*Aion*, CW 9ii, §14ff). Meeting it is the condition of becoming whole, not a moral exercise.

**Individuation** is the lifelong process of differentiating from the collective psyche and coming into conscious relation with the Self. It is integration, not ascent — and it produces a more related person, not a more isolated one. This matters for an inside-out organization: the inside-out move is not solipsism. Individuation makes a person more available to others, not less.

**Projection** is the mechanism by which the inside becomes the outside. What a person refuses to see in themselves returns to them wearing another face. Drawn to the collective scale, in *The Undiscovered Self* (1957), Jung was clear that society cannot be regenerated by bypassing the individuals who make it up. The only lever is the person who has done the interior work — whose being then radiates into the field around them.

### Quotes

- *"The psychological rule says that when an inner situation is not made conscious, it happens outside, as fate. That is to say, when the individual remains undivided and does not become conscious of his inner opposite, the world must perforce act out the conflict and be torn into opposing halves."* — *Aion*, CW 9ii, §126
- *"One does not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but by making the darkness conscious."* — "The Philosophical Tree" (1945), CW 13, §335
- *"Individuation is a process of differentiation, having for its goal the development of the individual personality."* — *Psychological Types*, CW 6, §757
- *"If the individual is not truly regenerated in spirit, society cannot be either, for society is the sum total of individuals in need of redemption."* — *The Undiscovered Self* (1957), CW 10

### Edges

**Robust and uncontested in serious reading:** the shadow, projection, the compensatory function of the unconscious, individuation as a lifelong asymmetric process, the ego–Self distinction, the clinical observation that what is repressed returns as symptom or fate.

**Contested:** the collective unconscious as a literal inherited substrate (many contemporary depth psychologists treat it as phenomenological rather than ontological); the archetypes as cross-cultural universals; the gendered anima/animus framing; synchronicity as causally meaningful. Jung's conduct and writings during the early 1930s regarding Germany are a real and ongoing stain that serious readers do not dismiss.

**Famously misattributed — do not use:**
- "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate." — a paraphrase of *Aion* §126 that has taken on a life of its own. Use the real §126 passage.
- "The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are." — almost certainly Joseph Campbell, not Jung.
- "What you resist, persists." — later self-help, not Jung.

## Gabor Maté (b. 1944)

### Seeing

Trauma is not the event. Trauma is what happened *inside* a person as a result of what happened to them — what their nervous system, their attachments, and their relational life did with it. This move relocates the wound from the story to the interior. It has two consequences. No one is disqualified from healing because their story is "not bad enough." And healing does not require rewriting history — only meeting what the interior is still holding.

A child is born with two non-negotiable needs: **attachment and authenticity**. When they collide — when staying connected to a caregiver requires leaving some true part of the self behind — the child chooses attachment, because survival depends on it. That choice was intelligent; it was not free. Decades later it shows up as addiction, as illness, as chronic performance, or as what Maté calls "the good person" — admired for traits that are actually the trauma speaking. The disease, the numbing, the compliance are the cost of the original deal.

Maté refuses to leave the wound private. What we call normal in industrialized societies is itself traumatizing — a culture that demands the suppression of authenticity in exchange for belonging. The personal, the relational, and the cultural are the same field, read at different scales. **Compassionate Inquiry**, his practice, is the slow body-attentive meeting of the moment the deal was made. Witness, not interpretation. The therapist tracks the place where disconnection from self first happened, and the person, often for the first time, lets what was exiled come back.

### Quotes

- *"Trauma is not what happens to you. It is what happens inside you as a result of what happens to you."* — *The Myth of Normal* (2022), and repeatedly in recorded talks
- *"The question is never 'Why the addiction?' but 'Why the pain?'"* — associated with *In the Realm of Hungry Ghosts* (2008)
- *"When authenticity threatens attachment, attachment trumps authenticity."* — on the child's dilemma, *The Myth of Normal*
- *"Safety is not the absence of threat; it is the presence of connection."* — recurring in talks; verify against a transcript before printing

### Edges

**Robust:** the clinical observation that addiction tracks pain and early relational wounding; the attachment/authenticity frame as a plain articulation of well-supported attachment theory; trauma-as-internal-response, which aligns with mainstream trauma science (van der Kolk, Porges, Levine).

**Contested:** the personality-to-cancer and chronic illness claims in *When the Body Says No* (2003) run ahead of the epidemiological evidence, and critics have warned that the frame risks subtly blaming sick people for their illness. The strong environmental thesis of ADHD in *Scattered Minds* dismisses heritability that the genetic literature supports (70–80% by mainstream estimates). The March 2023 livestream in which Maté publicly diagnosed Prince Harry at a distance from reading *Spare* drew sharp professional criticism and is the most recent reputational flag a sophisticated reader may know.

Use Maté for his framing of interior trauma and the attachment-authenticity dilemma. Do not use him as a citation for disease mechanism.

## Peter Levine (b. 1942)

### Seeing

Trauma is an incomplete self-protective response frozen in the body. The nervous system mobilizes enormous survival energy to fight or flee; when neither is possible, the body falls back on immobility — the freeze. In wild animals, the immobility is typically discharged: the body trembles, shakes, re-orients, and returns to baseline. Humans, uniquely, often *interrupt* that discharge — by cortical override, by shame, by chronic threat — and the mobilized energy stays bound in the tissues and in the autonomic pattern. Symptoms (hyperarousal, numbing, dissociation) are the still-ongoing survival response, not damage to be repaired.

The wild-animal observation is not a proven ethological claim — it is a naturalist's intuition Levine generalized into a working hypothesis about human nervous systems. And "frozen in the body" is a working phrase for autonomic and procedural memory patterns, not an anatomical location. The phenomenology of freeze and discharge is what is robust; the physiology is a metaphor for something real.

The method follows from the seeing. If the charge is still live, the work is to let it complete — but slowly, in doses the system can metabolize. Hence **titration**: small amounts of activation at a time. **Pendulation**: the natural rhythm of contraction and expansion, threat and settling, which Levine treats as already present in a healthy nervous system and something to be restored rather than installed. **Resource**: something — a sensation, an image, a relationship, a breath that comes back — that reliably brings a felt sense of okayness, accessed *first*, so there is ground to return to before any charge is touched.

The move is always toward **capacity before content**. Going straight at the trauma — catharsis, abreaction, reliving — is, in Levine's frame, retraumatization. His formulation: "too much, too fast, too soon" is the thing the work is built to avoid.

The lineage: Wilhelm Reich (character armor) and Alexander Lowen (bioenergetics) are the direct somatic ancestors. Stephen Porges (polyvagal theory) is Levine's dialogue partner. Bessel van der Kolk is the parallel figure in the same generation, reaching for different tools (EMDR, yoga, neurofeedback) but seeing much of the same territory.

### Quotes

- *"Trauma is a fact of life. It does not, however, have to be a life sentence."* — *Waking the Tiger* (1997)
- *"When we fight against and/or hide from unpleasant or painful sensations and feelings, we generally make things worse. The more we avoid them, the greater is the power they exert upon our behavior and sense of well-being."* — *In an Unspoken Voice* (2010)
- *"The body has been designed to renew itself through continuous self-correction. These same principles also apply to the healing of psyche, spirit, and soul."* — *Waking the Tiger*

### Edges

**Robust:** the phenomenology of freeze as a distinct third response beyond fight/flight; the clinical utility of slow exposure with resourcing; the general principle that trauma is physiologically encoded and not merely a memory problem — now close to consensus in trauma science, and broadly consonant with Porges's polyvagal work and van der Kolk's clinical observations.

**Contested:** Somatic Experiencing as a named protocol has an evidence base that is preliminary rather than established. The Kuhfuß et al. 2021 scoping review in the *European Journal of Psychotraumatology* — the first systematic review of SE — found 16 studies meeting inclusion, of which only two were rigorous RCTs. The most-cited (Brom et al. 2017, n=63) reported large effect sizes but remains a single small trial. The review's own summary: results are "promising" but "the current evidence base is weak and does not fully accomplish the high standards for clinical effectiveness research."

Guideline status: SE does not currently appear as a first-line recommended treatment in the ISTSS, APA, or WHO PTSD guidelines, which continue to place trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, and prolonged exposure at the top of the hierarchy. This is not the same as saying SE does not work — it means the RCT base needed for guideline-level recommendation has not yet been built. A writer standing on Levine is on firmer ground citing his phenomenology (freeze, titration, pendulation, resource) than citing SE efficacy numbers.

## Debbie Ford (1955–2013)

### Seeing

The parts of ourselves we refuse to own do not go quiet. They run the life from underneath. Ford's distinctive move, and the reason she matters as a voice of her own rather than a footnote to Jung: the shadow is not only a bag of wounds and shameful impulses. It also contains disowned *gifts* — brilliance, creativity, sexuality, leadership, anger-as-power. What we envy in others and what we condemn in others are both mirrors, pointing to something of ours we exiled. The life a person actually lives — the partners they choose, the ceiling they cannot break through, the pattern that keeps repeating — is shaped to the exact contour of what they cannot bear to see in themselves.

Healing, for Ford, is **reclamation**: bringing the exiled material home so it stops directing things from the basement. The reframing is practical. The work is no longer "what was done to me" but "what am I refusing to claim." That turn is what takes a person out of victimhood without erasing the hurt — and it is Ford's most durable contribution.

Her second seeing is about **story**. A person's identity congeals around a private narrative — *I am unworthy*, *I will be abandoned*, *I am too much*. The narrative is the shadow's voice masquerading as truth. Forgiveness, especially self-forgiveness, is how the story loosens its grip. Not as moral generosity toward another person, but as the act by which a person stops being imprisoned by their own past.

Her pedagogy was built for ordinary people, not clinicians: workshops, guided processes, journaling, group mirroring, ritual. She wagered that this work could be done in a weekend room by a reasonably prepared facilitator — a democratizing move that earned her reach, and that also names the method's limits.

### Quotes

- *"You must go into the dark in order to bring forth your light."* — *The Dark Side of the Light Chasers* (1998)
- *"All of your so-called faults, all the things which you don't like about yourself are your greatest assets. They are simply overamplified."* — *The Dark Side of the Light Chasers*
- *"When you don't own an aspect of yourself, it runs your life."* — *The Dark Side of the Light Chasers*
- *"Every day you don't forgive, it's as if you are ingesting tiny bits of poison."* — Ford taught this often and attributed it to Harold Bloomfield; cite the attribution honestly

### Edges

**Lineage.** The image of "the gold in the shadow" predates Ford. Jung held the substance (the shadow contains rejected gifts as well as rejected evils, *Aion* CW 9ii §14ff). Robert Bly popularized the image of the "long bag we drag behind us" and the work of reaching back into the bag for what is valuable, in *A Little Book on the Human Shadow* (1988) — a decade before Ford's first book. Ford inherited the frame and made it teachable. The honest lineage is Jung → Bly → Ford. Ford is the popularizer, not the originator. Honor what the popularization does without overclaiming where the concept comes from.

**Robust:** the projection insight is durable depth psychology; the shadow-as-power reframe is Ford's real contribution and lands practically in workshop rooms with non-clinical adults.

**Contested:** Ford is not a clinician. For people carrying significant trauma — war-displaced families, survivors of active violence — the workshop format can surface material the room is not equipped to hold. This is a real limit for a population like the one InsideOut serves. Use Ford as **language and permission** (reclaim your power, own your story, forgive yourself). Do not use her as a standalone method for heavily traumatized populations without a somatic container alongside.

## Eckhart Tolle (b. 1948)

### Seeing

There is a layer of mental activity — compulsive, self-referential, almost always running — that a person mistakes for themselves. Tolle calls this identification "ego." Ego is not the self; it is an attachment to a construct made of thought: roles, stories, grievances, self-image, the endless projection forward and back in time. Most suffering, in his reading, is not caused by events but by the mind's relation to events — resistance, commentary, the refusal of what is already here.

Alongside ego he names the **pain-body**: an accumulated field of unprocessed emotional pain that has become semi-autonomous, that feeds on more pain, that periodically takes a person over and drives them to seek or generate the situations that regenerate it. This is Tolle's most body-adjacent concept, though he describes it energetically rather than physiologically. Operationally, it is a precise description of how unprocessed emotional pain reasserts itself — not as memory, but as a mood that takes over and then generates the thoughts and reactions that keep it fed.

The exit he points to is **presence**: a shift of attention out of the thought-stream into direct awareness of this moment. The turn is not to think different thoughts. It is to become the one who notices thought — *watching the thinker*. **Surrender** is the corollary. Not passivity, not resignation — the inner dropping of the *no*, the cessation of resistance to what is already the case. From surrender, action becomes possible without the distortions ego adds. His large claim: collective awakening proceeds through individual awakening, and enough individuals becoming present changes the field.

Tolle's own stated lineage is Krishnamurti, Ramana Maharshi, Nisargadatta Maharaj, the Upanishads, the *Tao Te Ching*, and Meister Eckhart. Jung is not in it. Place him as thematic resonance with the depth-psychology voices, not as their descendant. The lineage behind him is a documented contemplative tradition with centuries of practice; naming it earns the vocabulary a credibility it does not have when presented as new-age improvisation.

### Quotes

- *"Not to be able to stop thinking is a dreadful affliction, but we don't realize this because almost everybody is suffering from it, so it is considered normal."* — *The Power of Now* (1997), Ch. 1
- *"The pain-body consists of trapped life-energy that has split off from your total energy field and has temporarily become autonomous."* — *The Power of Now*, Ch. 2
- *"In the Now, in the absence of time, all your problems dissolve. Suffering needs time; it cannot survive in the Now."* — *The Power of Now*, Ch. 3
- *"Surrender is the simple but profound wisdom of yielding to rather than opposing the flow of life."* — *The Power of Now*, Ch. 10
- *"Through surrender, spiritual energy comes into this world… it will create a different world when sufficient humans enter the surrendered state."* — *The Power of Now*, Ch. 10

### Edges

**Robust:** the phenomenology of thought-identification and of presence is reproducible — people across traditions report the same shift. The pain-body observation maps usefully onto clinical trauma work. Surrender as inner non-resistance is a solid contemplative move with long precedent.

**Contested:** the framework is experientially evocative rather than operationally defined. "Ego," "presence," "pain-body" cannot be measured, only gestured at. More importantly, the teaching is **structurally unfalsifiable** — disagreement with it can be read as the ego defending itself, which makes the frame resistant to correction. Name this honestly rather than import it.

Tolle has essentially no account of history, culture, power, or collective trauma. For an organization rooted in frontline humanitarian work with war-displaced families, this matters. Tolle can hold the inside of a Ukrainian mother's experience — the pain-body, the difficulty of presence when the body is still in survival mode — but he cannot hold *why she is in Gothenburg*. Use him for the interior shift, not for the situation. Pair; do not lean alone.

**A misattributed phrase to remove, if it appears in any InsideOut materials:** "You are the one dreaming the dream" is not Tolle. The real line from *A New Earth* is *"there is the dream, and there is the dreamer of the dream."* Many other "Tolle quotes" circulating online are user-submitted and unverifiable — verify against the books themselves.

## Joe Dispenza (b. 1962)

### Seeing

Identity is a habit. The familiar self — the one that rehearses yesterday's emotions on tomorrow's timeline — is a biologically inscribed loop: a body that has learned to produce the same chemistry in the same sequences so reliably that the person mistakes the loop for who they are. At the inside, this is continuous with what Maté means by being held in the past, and what Tolle means by the pain-body. A self that cannot meet the present because the present is filtered through stored affect.

Dispenza's distinctive move is the claim that the same mechanism that installs the loop can install something else. A vision of the future lived with enough vividness and emotional reality that the body begins to metabolize it as memory before it has happened. The rehearsal must be felt, not thought — the body has to *be there* emotionally before the neural pattern takes. On this reading, interior practice is not preparation for change. It *is* the change, at a biological level.

The practice architecture he teaches — sustained meditation, breath and energy work, sustained affective rehearsal of a future self — is designed as a rehearsal chamber for that installation. The seeing, held carefully, has real kinship with what the other five voices are pointing at. The liability is not the seeing. It is the packaging around it.

### Quotes

- *"When you think from your past memories, you can only create past experiences."* — *Breaking the Habit of Being Yourself* (2012)
- *"To sum up the meditative process, you have to break the habit of being yourself and reinvent a new self; lose your mind and create a new one."* — *Breaking the Habit of Being Yourself*

### Edges — held at arm's length

Because the packaging around Dispenza is the main reason he cannot be cited the way the other five can, the edges here are longer than the seeing, and that asymmetry is load-bearing.

**Credentials.** Doctor of Chiropractic (D.C.) from Life University, Georgia. No MD, no PhD in neuroscience. The "PhD" sometimes attached to his name traces to Quantum University in Honolulu, an unaccredited distance-learning school where he sits on the faculty. The honest titles are "D.C." or "chiropractor and author." Calling him a neuroscientist is not defensible, and a single Google search will undo whatever institutional credibility the citation was meant to carry.

**Affiliation.** Long-time student and appointed teacher at Ramtha's School of Enlightenment, founded by J. Z. Knight around her claim to channel a 35,000-year-old Atlantean warrior. He appeared as a talking head in the 2004 film *What the Bleep Do We Know?*, produced by people affiliated with Ramtha and widely regarded by working physicists as a misrepresentation of quantum mechanics. This affiliation is on the public record and travels with him into any room he enters.

**The research corpus, honestly.** Two peer-reviewed studies are usually cited. Church et al. 2022 in *Frontiers in Psychology* reported salivary IgA, qEEG, and psychological changes at a Dispenza workshop — with a weak active-placebo control (a Rumi-poetry condition that cannot blind participants to being at a workshop) and an allied rather than independent lead author. Jinich-Diamant et al. 2025 in *Communications Biology* reported neural and molecular changes at a 7-day retreat — n=20, open-label, no control arm, with Dispenza disclosed as a co-investigator through Encephalon, Inc. Both papers are real. Neither can carry the institutional weight that grant reviewers expect.

**The quantum-field language.** Working physicists have treated Dispenza's use of "quantum field," "observer effects," and related language as metaphor performing as mechanism. There is no established mechanism in physics by which individual contemplative intent couples to the quantum vacuum to produce macroscopic biological outcomes. When he speaks in this register, he is doing something other than reporting science. Cut every trace of it from any InsideOut outward-facing writing.

**Healing testimonials.** The retreats include accounts of recovery from cancer, MS, Parkinson's, infertility. Any suggestion in InsideOut's materials that interior practice is a treatment for such conditions would be the single largest liability the organization could take on. Swedish medical and grant-review culture will react sharply, and — more importantly — people in acute distress can take such claims literally and delay the medical care they need. This is not a stylistic question. Do not go near it.

**What to cite instead, for the phenomenon Dispenza points at.** There is a serious independent literature on sustained contemplative practice and measurable biological change, and it is the one InsideOut should build any formal measurement language on:

- **Richard Davidson and Antoine Lutz** (Wisconsin, Center for Healthy Minds) — neural correlates of meditation, long-term meditator EEG and gamma synchrony, compassion training.
- **Sara Lazar** (Harvard / MGH) — cortical thickness and structural neuroplasticity with mindfulness practice.
- **Amishi Jha** (Miami) — attention training in high-stress populations including military and first responders. Especially valuable because it demonstrates effects in exactly the kinds of populations humanitarian funders care about.
- **Judson Brewer** (Brown) — neural correlates of craving, habit, and default-mode network; contemplative approaches to addiction.
- **The Shamatha Project** (UC Davis, Saron and colleagues) — one of the most methodologically rigorous long-term meditation studies in existence.
- **Elissa Epel** (UCSF) — stress, telomere biology, and contemplative intervention.

This literature is peer-reviewed, independent of commercial retreat operators, and says — carefully — much of what Dispenza says loudly. For grants and formal writing, cite it. Dispenza can appear in this file as one voice among six, with his packaging named honestly. **He should not appear in grant applications as a scientific authority.**

## What the six see together

Held together, the voices point at a common territory. Different vocabularies, different disciplines, different methods — but the seeing underneath them is recognizable as one seeing. Four threads come through clearly.

**What is held inside a person shapes what comes out.** Jung: the unmet interior returns as fate. Maté: trauma is what happened *inside* you. Levine: bound survival energy keeps shaping the nervous system. Ford: disowned parts run the life from the basement. Tolle: the pain-body regenerates the conditions that feed it. Dispenza: the familiar self is a biological loop that produces its own future. Six ways of saying one thing.

**Symptoms are intelligence, not damage.** Each of them, in their register, refuses to read suffering as defect. The addiction is a solution to pain. The freeze is a completed survival response waiting to finish. The judgment of another is a mirror pointing back. The pain-body protects something that was once too much to feel. The shadow contains gold as well as lead. The habit of being yourself is how the body kept you alive. Meeting what is there, honestly, is the work — not repairing it.

**Inside and outside are one motion.** Personal and collective are the same field at different scales. Jung: society cannot be regenerated by bypassing the individuals who make it up. Maté: what we call normal is itself traumatizing, and the wound is cultural as well as personal. Tolle: collective awakening proceeds through individual presence. Dispenza: the internal state conditions the outer circumstance. They differ about *how* the motion runs. They all agree that it runs.

**Healing does not install. It uncovers.** The health a person needs is already in them, covered over by what they had to do to survive. Levine: the nervous system knows how to complete. Maté: authenticity is what was exchanged, not what was destroyed. Ford: the disowned parts are waiting. Tolle: presence is a return, not an arrival. Jung: individuation is becoming what one already, in latent form, is. Dispenza, at his best, is in this camp too — the body remembers coherence before it has to rehearse it.

At the inside, the hypothesis holds. These six are not arguing about the nature of what they have seen. They are translating it into the dialects their disciplines made available.

## The honest edges

Agreement at the core does not mean agreement everywhere. Three real navigation points survived the research, and the tool should hold them as navigation points rather than smooth them. None of them are contradictions at the inside level. All of them shape how the voices can be used safely with a population carrying real trauma load.

**1. Pace.** Ford's workshop reclamation and Dispenza's rehearsal both move at a speed that Levine's titration principle would read as dangerous for a highly activated nervous system. The partial resolution: Ford and Dispenza work mostly with cognitive-emotional and identity material, which can move faster than raw autonomic charge, because the defense being met is cognitive-emotional rather than a frozen survival response. But for a population with active trauma load, Levine's caution is load-bearing. Capacity before content. The tool should not let anyone forget this.

**2. Shadow or bypass.** Dispenza's future-self practice, done alone without shadow work first, risks producing exactly the dissociated positivity Ford wrote against. Ford is the correction, and the tool should keep them near each other. Seeing what is disowned is not optional before rehearsing what is becoming. A future-self rehearsed over an unmet shadow is brittle — it fails the moment life presses on it.

**3. Individual or relational.** Maté insists that suffering is relational all the way down — it happened in a field of other people, and it heals in a field of other people. Dispenza's practice can be done alone on a meditation cushion; Tolle has essentially no account of structure or history. For a humanitarian organization working with war-displaced families, this is not a vocabulary difference. It is a question about where the wound actually lives, and where *alone* is not sufficient. Jung's *Undiscovered Self* and Maté's entire frame hold the personal and collective together. Use them to hold Tolle and Dispenza from drifting individualist.

Two further tensions appeared during the research and turned out, on closer reading, to be smaller than they first looked — but worth naming so a writer knows where they stand:

- **Jung integrates the ego; Tolle dissolves identification with it.** At the endpoint this looks like a disagreement, and at the endpoint there is a real difference — Jung keeps the ego as a functioning organ of consciousness that must be related to, not seen through. But both are agreeing on the prior move: the ordinary "I" is not the whole, and *identification* is the problem. The difference is what a mature end-state looks like, not what the work is.
- **Emergence or selection.** Jung insists the image of who one is becoming must arise from the unconscious, not be chosen by the ego. Dispenza is more willing to let the person choose a future self and rehearse it. At the inside, if the practice is deep enough, selection and emergence may converge — the rehearsal that actually takes is the one that meets what is already latent. At the surface, Jung would call a willed future-self inflation. A writer using both voices should know the tension exists and navigate it on a case-by-case basis.

These edges are not failures of the hypothesis. They are where a tool has to be held with judgment — which is what a tool is for.

## Using the file

A few notes for anyone writing from this.

- **Read for the seeing before reaching for a quote.** The quotes are here so they can be verified, not so they can be dropped into paragraphs. If the writing does not first carry the seeing, the quote will not save it. If the seeing is there, the writing may not need the quote at all.
- **Match the register to the room.** For grants, policy documents, and institutional materials, the measurable-interior-change claim should stand on Davidson, Lutz, Lazar, Jha, Brewer, the Shamatha Project, and Epel — not on Dispenza. For public writing about interior work, the six voices here are the ground. These are different rooms, not different organizations.
- **Hold the navigation points rather than resolving them in advance.** Where a piece of writing takes a position on pace, on shadow-or-bypass, or on individual-or-relational, it should do so knowingly — not by accident.
- **This file is a tool, not a creed.** The six voices are available because they are available. If a seventh or eighth voice belongs here later — Bessel van der Kolk, Stephen Porges, Richard Schwartz (Internal Family Systems), Pat Ogden (sensorimotor psychotherapy), or a voice from the Eastern European or Ukrainian contemplative tradition that InsideOut's lineage actually emerges from — add it when it earns its place. The frame is inside-out; the voices are the ones that helped find the shape.
